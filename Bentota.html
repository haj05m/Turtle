Bentota Turtle Hatchery

All visitors to Bentota enjoy visiting the Bentota Turtle Hatchery Project. The picturesque golden sands that entice you to our island also offer numerous endangered turtle species, such the Olive Ridley and Leatherback turtles, an alluring nesting site. The project's objective is to preserve and safeguard the eggs against predators and other threats. To find out more about these beautiful and endangered sea creatures, go to the turtle hatchery in Bentota.

The preservation of the island's wildlife is of utmost importance because Sri Lanka is one of the world's key biodiversity hotspots. The problem is addressed by funding the survival of Sri Lanka's turtles at the several turtle hatcheries that dot the western coastline.

What happens at the hatchery?

  1. Beach Conservation-You can assist in these efforts by helping to maintain the beach clean and plant shelter plants, for example.
  2. Work in Hatchery-Collecting eggs from nests on the shore and reburying them within the hatchery, where they may safely hatch, makes up a significant portion of the hatchery's job.
  3. Night Patrol-Patrolling the beach at night is one of the hatcheries' programs, which not only serves to stop poachers but also allows visitors to see lovely female turtles approaching their nests.
  4. Education & Awareness-The Bentota Hatchery plays a significant role in raising awareness of the value of protecting these endangered species among locals and visitors.

Make sure to stop at the Hatchery during your Bentota sightseeing trip to see some of the rarest sea turtle species in existence. You may get a look at the five sea turtle species that have been designated as endangered by purchasing an access ticket for Rs. 500 (about $3) at the entrance.

What happens at the hatchery?

Don't pass up the chance to go to Bentota, Sri Lanka's Kosgoda Turtle Hatchery. Kosgoda beach is well known for protecting turtles. Every year, over 400 turtles lay their eggs on this beach. There are 18 turtle hatcheries total, and they are all situated on Sri Lanka's southern coast. Female turtles excavate holes where they will lay their eggs during the breeding season, covering the holes with sand to provide protection. The heat of the sun warms the eggs as they are laid beneath the sand.

However, private organizations like the Bentota Turtle hatchery have taken on the duty of gathering and burying these eggs in a sandpit within their facilities until they successfully hatch due to the threat of wild animals and people on the beach. When the turtles reach adulthood, they are appropriately released back into the wild. Additionally, ill and injured turtles are cared for in the turtle conservatory. In this turtle conservatory in Bentota, you may view numerous sea turtle species at various phases of development. Donations are used to maintain the conservatory.
