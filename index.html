Introduction

Sri Lanka’s sea turtles are considered a national gem. The five species found in Sri Lankan waters; Green,Leatherback,Olive Ridley,Loggerhead,Hawksbill. Sea turtles spend most of their lives in water, but come ashore occasionally to lay their eggs. Their local nesting areas in Sri Lanka stretch from Mount Lavinia, down the west coast, across the south coast, and up to Arugam Bay on the East coast.

Humans provide the greatest threat to sea turtles in Sri Lanka despite the fact that they have natural predators. The Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance of Sri Lanka makes it illegal to kill, injure, or otherwise cause harm to a turtle. Additionally, taking or destroying turtle eggs is a federal felony, as is owning or selling turtles. Unfortunately, harmful practices that affect these marine creatures still exist.
Particularly vulnerable are sea turtles that eat jellyfish; when they mistake floating plastic bags for food, the plastic in their guts kills them. Numerous national and international organizations work to conserve sea turtles and safeguard their numbers from these anthropogenic challenges. Sadly, Sri Lanka frequently lacks the funding necessary to maintain ongoing sea turtle conservation efforts at full strength.


The importance of temperature

Like many other turtle species, the temperature in the nest affects the sex of sea turtles. Male hatchlings are produced by cooler incubation temperatures, while female hatchlings are produced by warmer incubation temperatures. A mixture of male and female hatchlings will be produced in conditions that alternate between the two extremes.

According to research, male turtle hatchlings will emerge from a turtle's eggs if the temperature is below 27.7° Celsius (81.86° Fahrenheit). However, the hatchlings will be female if the eggs incubate at temperatures higher than 31° Celsius (88.8° Fahrenheit). A mixture of male and female young turtles will be born when temperatures are between the two extremes. Additionally, researchers have discovered that the proportion of female turtles is higher in warmer sand.

Poaching can also be avoided by assisting communities in finding other employment possibilities, such as ecotourism.
